# Lost Value in Waste: A Global Economic Opportunity

**Source:** Roland Berger Report (v6) | **Pages:** 20 | **Compression:** 1:10

## Executive Summary

This Roland Berger report quantifies the massive economic opportunity hidden in global waste management. The central finding: **USD 90-120 billion is lost annually** in material value alone from Municipal Solid Waste (MSW) that ends up in landfills or open dumps instead of being recovered. With the global circularity rate at only 7%, there is enormous potential for both economic value capture and environmental impact reduction.

---

## 1. A Global Emergency (p. 4)

The world faces an environmental crisis requiring sustainability as the top priority. Traditional linear economic models—extract, produce, consume, dispose—are fundamentally unsustainable.

**Key Statistics:**
- Global resource consumption costs **>USD 7 trillion annually** in environmental damage
- Global circularity rate: only **7%**
- USD 160-180 billion total value generated in MSW annually

### The Circular Economy Framework

The circular economy model operates across three pillars:

| Pillar | Focus | Goal |
|--------|-------|------|
| **Production** | Prevent waste creation | Alternative business models, sustainable design |
| **Management** | Keep materials circulating | Reuse, recycling, composting |
| **Leakage** | Maximize end-of-life value | Energy recovery, safe disposal |

### Four Types of Economic Value Loss

1. **Material Value** - Tradeable commodities (paper, plastics, glass, metal) lost to landfills
2. **Lost Energy Revenue** - Potential energy from Waste-to-Energy (WtE) systems
3. **Circular Economy Valorization** - Alternative applications and upcycling potential
4. **Externalities** - Direct and indirect environmental/social costs

### The Bottom Line

| Metric | Value |
|--------|-------|
| Total MSW value generated | USD 160-180 billion/year |
| Value lost to landfills/dumping | USD 90-120 billion/year (56-67%) |
| Net loss from WtE vs. recycling | USD 3-8 billion/year |

**Hidden Opportunity:** Precious metals in e-waste (Cobalt, Tin, Nickel) valued at >USD 20,000/ton highlight landfill mining potential.

## 4. Recommendations (pp. 15-16)

Roland Berger presents four strategic recommendations:

### 1. Establish Enablers
- Innovation ecosystems
- Sustainable funding mechanisms
- Infrastructure investment
- Supportive policy frameworks

### 2. Develop Global Monitoring Systems
- Track recyclable material flows internationally
- Ensure equitable fund distribution to developing regions
- Investment in Virtual Recycling Credit scheme (with ISWA)

### 3. Adopt Global Standard Terminology
- Prevent dilution of "circularity" definition
- Accurate "recycling rate" reporting
- Consistent measurement across jurisdictions

### 4. Encourage Global Cooperation
- Look beyond geographical/political boundaries
- Collective financing for developing nations
- Target: minimum Archetype 3 globally

### Natural Capital Accounting

> "Including natural capital values in companies' P&L accounting and annual financial accounts would help achieve true transparency about the degree of equilibrium between business and nature."

## Key Concepts Glossary

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **Circular Economy** | Sustainable model reducing waste through Production, Management, and Leakage optimization |
| **Circularity Rate** | % of material kept in circular economy vs. total material used (currently 7% globally) |
| **MSW** | Municipal Solid Waste - household and commercial waste |
| **WtE** | Waste-to-Energy systems converting waste to power |
| **Dirty MRF** | Materials Recovery Facility processing mixed/unsorted waste |
| **EPR** | Extended Producer Responsibility - producer accountability schemes |
| **DRS** | Deposit Return Schemes for recyclable containers |
| **Natural Capital Accounting** | Including environmental values in corporate financial statements |
| **Virtual Recycling Credit** | Global financing mechanism for recycling in developing regions |
