# Understanding the Three Horizons Framework
## A Strategic Planning Guide for Gebtron

---

## Introduction

The Three Horizons Framework was developed by McKinsey & Company consultants Mehrdad Baghai, Stephen Coley, and David White, first published in their 1999 book "The Alchemy of Growth."

This framework helps companies manage the tension between **running today's business** and **building tomorrow's**. It recognizes that organizations must simultaneously:

1. Protect and grow current revenue streams
2. Build emerging businesses that will become future core
3. Explore options that might be transformational

## Horizon 1: Core Business (0-2 years)

### What It Is

Horizon 1 represents **everything that generates revenue today**. These are mature or maturing businesses with:
- Established customers
- Proven products/services
- Predictable economics
- Known competitors

### Strategic Imperatives

| Imperative | Description | Example for Gebtron |
|------------|-------------|---------------------|
| **Defend** | Protect market position from competitors | Strengthen top 5 client relationships |
| **Extend** | Incrementally improve current offerings | Add maintenance contracts to equipment sales |
| **Optimize** | Improve efficiency and margins | Standardize engineering designs to reduce delivery time |
| **Extract** | Generate cash to fund H2 and H3 | Improve project margins by 2% through better procurement |

### Characteristics

| Aspect | Horizon 1 |
|--------|-----------|
| Time Frame | 0-2 years |
| Objective | Maximize profitability of existing business |
| Metrics | Revenue, profit margin, market share, customer retention |
| Risk Level | Low (known business) |
| Management Style | Operational excellence, process improvement |
| Resource Allocation | ~70% of resources |

### Key Questions to Ask

1. What products/services generate 80% of our revenue?
2. Which clients are most valuable? Are any at risk of leaving?
3. Where are we vulnerable to competitive attack?
4. How can we improve margins without losing quality?
5. What operational bottlenecks slow us down?

### For Gebtron Specifically

**Horizon 1 includes:**
- Bulk material handling equipment for cement industry
- Conveying systems (belt, screw, chain, pneumatic)
- Storage systems (silos, hoppers, bins)
- System integration services for existing clients (Lafarge, TITAN, Arabian Cement)
- Egypt and UAE markets

**Defend/Extend Actions:**
- Dedicated account management for top clients
- Capture more aftermarket revenue (spare parts, maintenance)
- Improve project delivery speed through standardization

### Warning Signs That H1 Is At Risk

- Market share declining
- Key clients exploring alternatives
- Margins shrinking due to competition
- Best employees leaving for competitors
- Technology becoming outdated

## Horizon 2: Emerging Opportunities (2-5 years)

### What It Is

Horizon 2 represents **businesses that have proven their potential but aren't yet at scale**. They've moved beyond pure experimentation—there's evidence of market demand and viable business model—but they require significant investment to reach full potential.

### Strategic Imperatives

| Imperative | Description | Example for Gebtron |
|------------|-------------|---------------------|
| **Build** | Develop capabilities, team, infrastructure | Hire dedicated alternative fuels engineering team |
| **Scale** | Grow revenue and market presence aggressively | Target 10 alternative fuels projects by 2026 |
| **Learn** | Refine model based on market feedback | Document learnings from every EAAS installation |
| **Prove** | Demonstrate this can become H1 business | Show alternative fuels can be 30% of revenue by 2028 |

### Characteristics

| Aspect | Horizon 2 |
|--------|-----------|
| Time Frame | 2-5 years |
| Objective | Build and scale to become next core business |
| Metrics | Revenue growth rate, customer acquisition, market validation |
| Risk Level | Medium (proven concept, unproven scale) |
| Management Style | Entrepreneurial within corporate structure |
| Resource Allocation | ~20% of resources |

### The Challenge of Horizon 2

H2 businesses feel uncomfortable because:
- Too big to ignore, too small for full organizational attention
- Compete with H1 for resources and talent
- Need different metrics than H1 (growth vs. profitability)
- Require entrepreneurial leadership, not just operational management

### Key Questions to Ask

1. What emerging opportunities show early traction?
2. What would it take to 10x this opportunity?
3. What capabilities must we build?
4. Who should lead this (entrepreneur mindset)?
5. How do we protect H2 investments from H1 demands?

### For Gebtron Specifically

**Horizon 2 includes:**
- Alternative fuels handling systems (RDF, SRF, biomass)
- EAAS (Energy-As-A-Service) model for equipment
- Saudi Arabia market expansion
- Waste-to-energy integration
- Circular economy positioning (waste → RDF → cement factory)

**Build/Scale Actions:**
- Hire 3-5 dedicated alternative fuels engineers
- Sign first 3 EAAS contracts as proof points
- Establish Saudi Arabia office/partnership
- Develop standard EAAS contract templates

### Signs That H2 Is Ready to Graduate to H1

- Revenue growing consistently 30%+ per year
- Repeatable sales process established
- Unit economics proven at scale
- Dedicated team can operate independently
- Contributes >15% of total revenue

## Horizon 3: Future Bets (5+ years)

### What It Is

Horizon 3 represents **future possibilities that are currently uncertain but could become significant**. These are small experiments, research projects, or strategic explorations that might become H2 opportunities in the future.

### Strategic Imperatives

| Imperative | Description | Example for Gebtron |
|------------|-------------|---------------------|
| **Explore** | Investigate potential without large commitment | Research hydrogen economy implications |
| **Experiment** | Run small pilots to validate assumptions | Pilot IoT sensors on 2-3 installations |
| **Create Options** | Develop relationships and knowledge for future | Build relationships with hydrogen developers |
| **Maintain Optionality** | Don't over-commit; be ready to pivot | Limit investment to 10% of resources |

### Characteristics

| Aspect | Horizon 3 |
|--------|-----------|
| Time Frame | 5+ years |
| Objective | Create options for future growth |
| Metrics | Learning, optionality, strategic insights |
| Risk Level | High (most will fail, few could transform) |
| Management Style | Visionary, experimental, venture capital mindset |
| Resource Allocation | ~10% of resources |

### The Nature of Horizon 3

H3 investments are like venture capital:
- Most will fail
- A few could be transformational
- Goal is learning and optionality, not immediate returns
- Success = validated learning, not revenue

### Key Questions to Ask

1. What trends could reshape our industry in 10 years?
2. What technologies might disrupt how we do business?
3. What are forward-thinking clients asking about?
4. What are global leaders investing in that we should watch?
5. How do we know when to promote H3 to H2?

### For Gebtron Specifically

**Potential Horizon 3 explorations:**

| Exploration | Why It Matters |
|-------------|----------------|
| **IoT/Predictive Maintenance** | Remote monitoring as a service could be new revenue stream |
| **Hydrogen Economy** | Hydrogen storage/handling could need bulk material systems |
| **Carbon Capture** | Cement is major CO2 emitter; material handling for capture systems |
| **AI/ML for Material Flow** | Optimization software could become product |
| **Training Academy** | Monetize engineering knowledge differently |
| **Sub-Saharan Africa** | Long-term geographic expansion |

**Explore Actions:**
- Attend 2-3 conferences on Industry 4.0 and cement decarbonization
- Pilot IoT sensors on one client installation
- Research hydrogen project developers in Gulf
- Allocate 1 engineer part-time to future technology research

### Signs That H3 Should Graduate to H2

- Clear market demand validated
- Business model hypothesis proven
- Path to scale identified
- Champion identified to lead scaling
- Required capabilities understood

### Signs That H3 Should Be Killed

- No market interest after 12-18 months of exploration
- Technology proves unfeasible
- Better alternatives discovered
- Strategic priorities shifted
- Cannot find internal champion

## Resource Allocation Across Horizons

### Typical Guidelines

| Resource Type | Horizon 1 | Horizon 2 | Horizon 3 |
|---------------|-----------|-----------|-----------|
| Financial Investment | 70% | 20% | 10% |
| Management Attention | 60% | 30% | 10% |
| Best Talent Placement | Core operations | H2 growth initiatives | Small exploration team |

### Why This Allocation Matters

**Common Mistakes:**

| Mistake | Consequence |
|---------|-------------|
| **Over-investing in H1** | Milking the core while future erodes; competitors catch up |
| **Under-investing in H1** | Core revenue declines; no cash to fund H2/H3 |
| **No H3 at all** | No pipeline for future; caught off-guard by disruption |
| **H2 starvation** | Emerging businesses die; never develop next core |
| **Too much H3** | Lots of experiments, nothing scales; distracted organization |

### Protecting H2 and H3 During Tough Times

When H1 faces pressure, the instinct is to cut H2/H3. This is dangerous because:
- H2/H3 represent the future
- Cutting them creates long-term vulnerability
- Competitors who maintain investment will leapfrog

**Protection Mechanisms:**
1. Ring-fence H2/H3 budgets
2. Separate H2/H3 reporting from H1 metrics
3. CEO explicitly sponsors H2/H3 initiatives
4. Set minimum investment thresholds that survive budget cuts

```

(Explore)                    (Build & Scale)              (Defend & Extend)
    │                             │                            │
    ▼                             ▼                            ▼
┌──────────────┐           ┌──────────────┐           ┌──────────────┐
│ IoT Sensors  │           │ Alternative  │           │ Cement Bulk  │
│ Pilot        │──────────▶│ Fuels at     │──────────▶│ Handling     │
│              │  Success  │ Scale        │  Maturity │ Equipment    │
└──────────────┘           └──────────────┘           └──────────────┘
                                 │
┌──────────────┐           ┌──────────────┐
│ Hydrogen     │           │ EAAS Model   │
│ Research     │──────────▶│ Scaling      │
```

**The Lifecycle:**
- H3 experiments that work → Graduate to H2
- H2 businesses that scale → Become new H1
- H1 businesses that decline → Eventually divest or wind down

**Healthy Portfolio:**
- Multiple H3 experiments running (expect most to fail)
- 1-3 H2 opportunities being scaled
- Strong H1 generating cash to fund the pipeline

## Different Metrics for Different Horizons

### Why One-Size-Fits-All Metrics Fail

If you measure H2/H3 by H1 metrics (profit, ROI), you'll kill them prematurely. Each horizon needs appropriate metrics.

### Horizon-Appropriate Metrics

| Horizon | Primary Metrics | Secondary Metrics |
|---------|-----------------|-------------------|
| **H1** | Revenue, Profit margin, Market share | Customer retention, NPS, Project on-time delivery |
| **H2** | Revenue growth rate, Customer acquisition cost, Market validation | Unit economics improvement, Team capability building |
| **H3** | Hypotheses tested, Learning velocity, Options created | Partnerships formed, Technical feasibility validated |

### Example: EAAS Model Metrics

**Wrong (H1 metrics):** "EAAS model isn't profitable yet, cut it"
**Right (H2 metrics):** "EAAS model signed 3 pilots, customer feedback positive, unit economics improving—continue scaling"

## Leadership Requirements by Horizon

### Different Horizons Need Different Leaders

| Horizon | Leadership Style | Key Skills |
|---------|------------------|------------|
| **H1** | Operational excellence | Process optimization, efficiency, reliability, execution |
| **H2** | Entrepreneurial | Scaling, team building, market development, adaptability |
| **H3** | Visionary/Explorer | Pattern recognition, curiosity, network building, experimentation |

### Common Mistake

Assigning H1 operational leaders to H2/H3 initiatives. They will:
- Apply H1 metrics too early
- Kill experiments before they mature
- Prioritize efficiency over learning
- Struggle with ambiguity

### Better Approach

- Identify entrepreneurial talent for H2
- Give H3 to curious explorers who can tolerate failure
- Keep operational leaders focused on H1 excellence

## Governance Structure

### Separate Review Cadences

| Horizon | Review Frequency | Review Focus |
|---------|------------------|--------------|
| **H1** | Monthly | Operational performance, P&L, project status |
| **H2** | Quarterly | Growth metrics, capability building, market progress |
| **H3** | Semi-annually | Learning, hypothesis validation, go/no-go decisions |

### Decision Rights

| Decision | Who Decides |
|----------|-------------|
| H1 operational decisions | Department heads |
| H2 investment decisions | CEO + designated H2 sponsor |
| H3 experiment approvals | CEO or innovation committee |
| Horizon promotions (H3→H2, H2→H1) | Leadership team |

## Key Takeaways

1. **All three horizons matter** — Neglecting any one creates vulnerability
2. **Different metrics for different horizons** — Don't judge H2/H3 by H1 standards
3. **Resource allocation is strategic** — 70/20/10 is a starting point, not a rule
4. **Leadership matters** — Match leader type to horizon requirements
5. **Protect the future** — Ring-fence H2/H3 from H1 budget pressure
6. **Manage the pipeline** — Actively graduate or kill initiatives between horizons
