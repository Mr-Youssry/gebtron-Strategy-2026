# Letter of Credit (LC) - Comprehensive Guide

## What is a Letter of Credit?

A **Letter of Credit (LC)**, also known as a documentary credit, is a financial instrument issued by a bank on behalf of a buyer (importer) that guarantees payment to a seller (exporter) upon the presentation of specified documents that comply with the terms and conditions set out in the LC.

### Key Purpose
- **Risk Mitigation**: Reduces payment risk in international trade
- **Trust Building**: Acts as a trusted intermediary between buyer and seller
- **Payment Guarantee**: Ensures seller receives payment if terms are met
- **Trade Facilitation**: Enables business between parties who may not know each other

---

## Key Parties Involved

### 1. **Applicant (Buyer/Importer)**
- Requests the LC from their bank
- Responsible for paying the bank

### 2. **Beneficiary (Seller/Exporter)**
- Receives payment under the LC
- Must comply with all LC terms and conditions

### 3. **Issuing Bank**
- The buyer's bank that issues the LC
- Commits to pay if documents comply with LC terms

### 4. **Advising Bank**
- Notifies the beneficiary of the LC
- Usually located in the seller's country
- Does not commit to pay (unless also confirming)

### 5. **Confirming Bank** (Optional)
- Adds its payment guarantee to the LC
- Provides additional security for the seller

### 6. **Negotiating Bank** (Optional)
- Examines documents and pays the beneficiary
- Seeks reimbursement from the issuing bank

---

## Types of Letters of Credit

### By Payment Terms

#### 1. **Sight LC**
- Payment made immediately upon presentation of compliant documents
- Fastest payment method

#### 2. **Deferred Payment LC**
- Payment made at a specified future date
- No draft is required

#### 3. **Acceptance LC**
- A draft is drawn and accepted for payment at maturity
- Creates a negotiable instrument

#### 4. **Negotiation LC**
- Bank may purchase/discount documents before maturity
- Provides immediate funds to the seller

### By Security Level

#### 5. **Irrevocable LC**
- Cannot be amended or canceled without all parties' consent
- Standard in modern trade (default under UCP 600)

#### 6. **Revocable LC** (Rare/Obsolete)
- Can be amended or canceled by the issuing bank without notice
- Not recommended due to lack of security

#### 7. **Confirmed LC**
- Payment guaranteed by both issuing and confirming banks
- Maximum security for the seller

#### 8. **Unconfirmed LC**
- Only the issuing bank's payment commitment
- Lower cost but higher risk for seller

### Special Types

#### 9. **Standby LC (SBLC)**
- Acts as a backup payment mechanism
- Similar to a bank guarantee
- Used for performance bonds, bid bonds, etc.

#### 10. **Revolving LC**
- Automatically renews for a specified period
- Useful for regular shipments between parties

#### 11. **Transferable LC**
- Can be transferred to one or more secondary beneficiaries
- Used when middlemen are involved

#### 12. **Back-to-Back LC**
- Two separate LCs linked together
- First LC used as collateral for second LC

#### 13. **Red Clause LC**
- Allows advance payment to beneficiary before shipment
- Used when seller needs funds for production

#### 14. **Green Clause LC**
- Similar to red clause but allows advances against warehouse receipts

## How a Letter of Credit Works

### Step-by-Step Process

```
1. Sales Contract
   ↓
   Buyer and Seller agree on terms including LC payment

2. LC Application
   ↓
   Buyer applies to their bank (Issuing Bank) for an LC

3. LC Issuance
   ↓
   Issuing Bank issues LC and sends to Advising Bank

4. LC Notification
   ↓
   Advising Bank notifies Seller and authenticates the LC

5. Shipment
   ↓
   Seller ships goods according to LC terms

6. Document Preparation
   ↓
   Seller prepares required documents (invoice, B/L, etc.)

7. Document Presentation
   ↓
   Seller presents documents to Negotiating/Advising Bank

8. Document Examination
   ↓
   Bank examines documents for compliance (5-7 business days)

9. Payment to Seller
   ↓
   If compliant, bank pays the Seller

10. Document Forwarding
    ↓
    Documents sent to Issuing Bank

11. Buyer Payment
    ↓
    Issuing Bank debits Buyer's account

12. Document Delivery
    ↓
    Buyer receives documents to claim goods
```

## Required Documents

### Standard Documents (Vary by LC)

1. **Commercial Invoice**
   - Details of goods, prices, terms
   - Must match LC description exactly

2. **Bill of Lading (B/L)**
   - Proof of shipment
   - Title document for goods
   - Types: Ocean, Air, Multimodal

3. **Packing List**
   - Details of packaging and contents

4. **Certificate of Origin**
   - Declares country of manufacture
   - May be required for customs/preferential tariffs

5. **Insurance Certificate/Policy**
   - Required if LC specifies CIF/CIP terms
   - Must cover the LC amount + percentage

6. **Inspection Certificate**
   - Third-party quality/quantity verification
   - Common in commodity trades

7. **Other Documents** (as specified)
   - Phytosanitary certificates
   - Weight certificates
   - Analysis certificates
   - Beneficiary's certificate

## Key Terms and Concepts

### UCP 600
**Uniform Customs and Practice for Documentary Credits**
- Published by International Chamber of Commerce (ICC)
- Standard rules governing LCs worldwide
- Current version: UCP 600 (effective 2007)

### ISBP
**International Standard Banking Practice**
- Supplement to UCP 600
- Provides guidelines for document examination

### Documentary Compliance
- Documents must strictly comply with LC terms
- "Doctrine of strict compliance" applies
- Even minor discrepancies can lead to rejection

### Discrepancies
Common discrepancies include:
- Late presentation of documents
- Expired LC
- Description of goods doesn't match exactly
- Document dates inconsistent
- Signature missing
- Amount exceeds LC value

### 5-Day Examination Period
- Banks have maximum 5 banking days to examine documents
- Stated in UCP 600, Article 14

## Advantages of Letters of Credit

### For Sellers (Exporters)
✅ **Payment Security**: Bank guarantee of payment
✅ **Credit Risk Mitigation**: Bank substitutes its creditworthiness
✅ **Reduced Country Risk**: Especially with confirmed LC
✅ **Financing Options**: Can use LC to obtain pre-shipment or post-shipment financing
✅ **Competitive Advantage**: More willing buyers when offering secure terms

### For Buyers (Importers)
✅ **Delivery Assurance**: Payment only upon document compliance
✅ **Quality Control**: Can require inspection certificates
✅ **Credit Terms**: Can negotiate deferred payment
✅ **Trade Finance**: Can obtain financing against LC
✅ **Trust Building**: Demonstrates creditworthiness to new suppliers

---

## Disadvantages and Challenges

### For Sellers
❌ **Complex Documentation**: Strict compliance required
❌ **Discrepancy Risk**: Minor errors can delay payment
❌ **Cost**: LC fees and bank charges
❌ **Time Consuming**: Documentation and processing time
❌ **Amendment Challenges**: Changes require buyer cooperation

### For Buyers
❌ **Cost**: Application fees, confirmation fees, amendment fees
❌ **Credit Line**: LC ties up credit facilities
❌ **Complexity**: Requires expertise to draft proper terms
❌ **Cash Flow**: May require margin/collateral deposit
❌ **Inflexibility**: Amendments can be difficult and costly

## Costs Involved

### Common LC Fees

1. **Issuance Fee**
   - Typically 0.1% - 2% of LC amount
   - Charged by issuing bank

2. **Advising Fee**
   - Flat fee ($50 - $200 typically)
   - Charged by advising bank

3. **Confirmation Fee**
   - 0.5% - 2% per quarter
   - Depends on country risk

4. **Negotiation Fee**
   - 0.1% - 0.5% of documents value
   - Charged by negotiating bank

5. **Amendment Fee**
   - $50 - $200 per amendment
   - Charged by issuing and advising banks

6. **Discrepancy Fee**
   - $50 - $150 per set of discrepancies
   - Charged if documents don't comply

## Best Practices

### For Buyers/Applicants

1. **Clear LC Terms**
   - Be specific but not overly restrictive
   - Use standard trade terms (Incoterms)
   - Avoid ambiguous language

2. **Realistic Timelines**
   - Allow sufficient time for production and shipment
   - Consider document preparation time
   - Include buffer for amendments

3. **Standard Documents**
   - Request only necessary documents
   - Use standard forms and formats
   - Avoid unusual requirements

4. **Professional Advice**
   - Consult with your bank's trade finance department
   - Use experienced freight forwarders
   - Consider legal review for large transactions

### For Sellers/Beneficiaries

1. **Review LC Immediately**
   - Check all terms upon receipt
   - Verify you can comply with all requirements
   - Request amendments ASAP if needed

2. **Document Accuracy**
   - Ensure exact compliance with LC terms
   - Double-check all details (amounts, descriptions, dates)
   - Use LC wording exactly in documents

3. **Early Preparation**
   - Prepare documents as soon as goods ship
   - Allow time for corrections
   - Present documents well before expiry

4. **Professional Assistance**
   - Work with experienced shipping/documentation staff
   - Use reliable freight forwarders
   - Consult with your bank before presentation

---

## Common Pitfalls to Avoid

### ⚠️ For Both Parties

1. **Late Presentation**
   - Present documents before expiry date
   - Consider transit time for courier

2. **Inconsistent Documents**
   - All documents must be consistent with each other
   - Dates should be logical (shipping after invoice, etc.)

3. **Partial Shipments**
   - Clarify if allowed or prohibited
   - Default: allowed under UCP 600

4. **Transhipment**
   - Specify if allowed or prohibited
   - Default: allowed under UCP 600

5. **Description Mismatch**
   - Goods description in invoice must match LC exactly
   - Other documents can use general descriptions

6. **Insurance Coverage**
   - Must cover LC amount plus 10% minimum
   - Must be in LC currency
   - Must be dated before/on shipment date

## Regulatory Framework

### International Rules
- **UCP 600**: Uniform Customs and Practice for Documentary Credits
- **ISBP**: International Standard Banking Practice
- **ISP98**: International Standby Practices (for SBLCs)
- **URR 725**: Uniform Rules for Bank-to-Bank Reimbursements

### Governing Law
- LCs are subject to rules chosen in the credit
- Usually UCP 600
- Local banking regulations also apply
- Dispute resolution typically through ICC or local courts

## Glossary of Terms

**Applicant**: The party applying for the LC (buyer/importer)

**Beneficiary**: The party receiving payment under the LC (seller/exporter)

**Clean B/L**: Bill of Lading with no adverse remarks about goods condition

**Complying Presentation**: Documents that comply with LC terms

**Discrepancy**: Any difference between LC terms and presented documents

**Expiry Date**: Last date for document presentation

**On Board**: Notation on B/L confirming goods loaded on vessel

**Reimbursement**: Payment from issuing bank to paying/negotiating bank

**Stale B/L**: Bill of Lading presented too late (usually >21 days after shipment)

**Tenor**: Time period for payment (e.g., sight, 30 days, 90 days)

**UCP 600**: Current international rules for documentary credits

## Conclusion

Letters of Credit remain one of the most important instruments in international trade, providing security and trust between parties across borders. While they involve costs and complexity, the risk mitigation they provide often makes them the preferred payment method for significant international transactions.

**Key Takeaways:**
1. LCs protect both buyers and sellers when properly structured
2. Strict compliance with terms is essential
3. Professional advice and careful documentation prevent issues
4. Understanding costs helps in pricing and negotiation
5. Digital transformation is improving efficiency

For successful use of LCs, invest time in understanding the process, work with experienced professionals, and maintain meticulous attention to detail in all documentation.
