# EAAS Financial Feasibility in Africa

> Does the $1M/year savings, zero-CapEx value proposition hold in Sub-Saharan Africa? Short answer: yes, with adjustments.

*⏱ 10 min read*
**Audience:** CEO, Finance Team, DFI Partners
**Date:** April 15, 2026

---

## The Bottom Line

The EAAS model works in Africa. Delivered coal costs ($130-200/t) are equal to or higher than MENA benchmarks. Labor costs are 30-70% lower. The savings range is **$0.7-1.5M/year** per line. The biggest risks are **waste quality** (high organic content), **currency** (outside CFA zone), and **waste collection infrastructure** (no tipping fee culture).

---

## 1. Energy Prices by Country

### Delivered Coal (Imported from South Africa)

Most African cement plants import thermal coal from Richards Bay (South Africa) or Mozambique. The benchmark is API4 FOB Richards Bay at **$109/t** (March 2026).

| Country | Delivered Price ($/t) | Confidence | Notes |
|---------|----------------------|------------|-------|
| **Senegal** | $140-165 | High | IFC confirmed imports from SA for Sococim, CIMAF, Dangote |
| **Cote d'Ivoire** | $140-170 | Medium | Via Abidjan port |
| **Kenya** | $130-160 | Medium | Via Mombasa; 2.4% excise duty adds cost |
| **Nigeria** | $140-170 | Medium | Lagos/Ogun plants; poor port infrastructure adds costs |
| **Tanzania** | $130-160 | Medium | Via Dar es Salaam; some low-quality domestic deposits |
| **Ethiopia** | $160-200 | Medium | Landlocked; coal via Djibouti + 700km road |
| **Uganda** | $180-220 | Low | Landlocked; coal via Mombasa + 1,000km road |
| **Cameroon** | $140-170 | Medium | Via Douala port |

**Key insight:** Landlocked countries (Ethiopia, Uganda) have the highest delivered coal costs — strongest EAAS savings case but also highest operational complexity.

### HFO and Natural Gas

| Fuel | Price | Relevance |
|------|-------|-----------|
| HFO | ~$470/t ($12/GJ) | Used by some African plants; EAAS even more attractive vs. HFO |
| Natural gas (Nigeria) | $2-4/GJ | Cheap but unreliable supply; many plants have dual-fuel capability |
| Pet coke | ~$172/t ($5.4/GJ) | Increasingly available from Dangote Refinery in Nigeria |

### Energy Subsidies — Mostly Absent

| Country | Subsidies? | Impact on EAAS |
|---------|-----------|----------------|
| **Kenya** | None + coal excise duty | **Best for EAAS** — fossil fuels are more expensive than market |
| **Senegal** | Electricity subsidies ending 2025; no coal subsidy | Good — post-reform creates opportunity window |
| **Ghana** | Moderate reform progress | Good — moving toward market pricing |
| **Tanzania** | Limited; broadly market-based | Good |
| **Nigeria** | Volatile — removed 2023, partially reinstated 2024 | Mixed — policy instability creates pricing uncertainty |
| **Ethiopia** | Cheap hydro electricity; imported coal at market price | Mixed — cement industry pays market for coal |

**Strategic conclusion:** Sub-Saharan Africa accounts for <3% of global fossil fuel subsidies (IMF data). Most SSA countries are net fuel importers paying close to market prices. This is fundamentally favorable for EAAS.

## 2. EAAS Economics — Africa vs. MENA

| Parameter | MENA Reference | Africa Range | Notes |
|-----------|---------------|-------------|-------|
| Delivered coal cost | $100-150/t | **$130-200/t** | Africa generally HIGHER |
| RDF production cost | $40-60/t | **$30-50/t** | Africa LOWER (cheaper labor, free waste) |
| Annual client savings | ~$1M | **$0.7-1.5M** | Varies by coal cost and utilization |
| EAAS annual revenue | ~$2M | **$1.5-2.5M** | Higher in landlocked countries |
| 15-year total profit | ~$15M | **$10-20M** | Currency-adjusted for CFA zone |
| CapEx per line | ~$3M | **$2.5-3.5M** | Slightly higher logistics to Africa |
| Labor (monthly) | $800-1,500 (Egypt) | **$200-600** | Significant savings |

**The math works.** Higher coal costs + lower labor costs = EAAS margins in Africa are comparable to or better than MENA.

## 3. The Organic Fraction Problem

This is the **single most important technical adaptation** for African EAAS.

### The Problem

African MSW is 55-70% organic (food waste) with high moisture content:

| Region | Organic % | Moisture | Calorific Value (raw) |
|--------|-----------|----------|----------------------|
| Europe | 20-30% | Low | 10-14 MJ/kg |
| MENA (Egypt) | 30-40% | Moderate | 8-11 MJ/kg |
| **Africa (SSA)** | **55-70%** | **High** | **5-10 MJ/kg** |

### Impact on EAAS Economics

| Standard Assumption | African Reality | Impact |
|--------------------|-----------------|--------|
| MSW-to-RDF conversion: 5:1 | Likely **7:1 or 8:1** | Need 40-60% more MSW feedstock |
| Annual MSW requirement: 800K t/yr | **1.1-1.3M t/yr** | Larger catchment area needed |
| RDF calorific value: 16-18 MJ/kg | **14-16 MJ/kg** | Slightly lower thermal substitution per ton |
| CapEx: $3M | **$3.2-3.5M** (add bio-drying) | $200-400K bio-drying stage |

### Mitigation Strategies

1. **Bio-drying (aerobic treatment):** 7-14 days of controlled biological treatment reduces moisture from 40-60% to 15-20%. Adds $200-400K to CapEx but dramatically improves RDF quality.

2. **C&D waste blending:** Construction and demolition waste has higher caloric value. Blending with MSW improves overall RDF quality. Particularly available in fast-urbanizing African cities.

3. **Super RDF:** Gebtron's cooking oil additive innovation — increases caloric value per ton. Egypt-proven technology transfers directly.

4. **Aged waste mining:** Old landfill waste (Mbeubeuss in Senegal: 50+ years; Dandora in Kenya: 30+ years) has lower moisture after decomposition. Higher caloric value than fresh MSW.

5. **Seasonal production adjustment:** Dry season waste has lower moisture. Adjust production intensity seasonally — maximize during dry season, reduce during rainy season.

### The Reppie Cautionary Tale

Ethiopia's Reppie plant (Africa's first WtE, $120M, 1,400 t/day) has been largely non-operational since 2018 commissioning. Relevant lessons:

| Reppie Problem | Why It Failed | Why EAAS Is Different |
|----------------|-------------|----------------------|
| Waste too wet for incineration | Designed for European waste profiles | RDF production allows pre-processing and quality control |
| Inconsistent waste supply | No long-term supply agreements | EAAS model includes MSW supply contracts |
| Complex technology | Direct incineration | Mechanical processing (shredding, screening) is simpler |
| Single large bet ($120M) | One plant, one city | EAAS lines at $3M each — portfolio approach |
| No local O&M | Foreign-built, no local team | Fort model provides year-round engineering team |

**RDF production for cement kiln co-processing is fundamentally different from direct incineration.** The cement kiln already exists, already handles variable fuel quality, and already has environmental permits. Gebtron's EAAS model avoids Reppie's core failure mode.

## 4. Currency and Payment Risk

### Currency Stability Matrix

| Country | Currency | 15-Year Risk | Recommended Contract Currency |
|---------|----------|-------------|------------------------------|
| **Senegal** | CFA (XOF) | **None** — EUR peg since 1945 | EUR-denominated |
| **Cote d'Ivoire** | CFA (XOF) | **None** | EUR-denominated |
| **Cameroon** | CFA (XAF) | **None** | EUR-denominated |
| **Kenya** | KES | Moderate (5-8%/yr depreciation) | USD-indexed with escalation clause |
| **Ghana** | GHS | High (20%+ in 2022) | USD-indexed; consider partial EUR |
| **Tanzania** | TZS | Moderate | USD-indexed |
| **Nigeria** | NGN | **Extreme** (70%+ loss in 2023-24) | USD mandatory; free zone entity |
| **Ethiopia** | ETB | **Extreme** (multiple devaluations; forex shortages) | **Avoid long-term local currency contracts** |

### Recommendation

**Start in CFA zone.** The 15-year EAAS contract is a currency bet. In the CFA zone, that bet is on the EUR (pegged since 1945, backed by French Treasury). In Nigeria, it's a bet on NGN stability over 15 years — which is a losing bet historically.

## 6. No Tipping Fee Culture — Revenue Model Implication

Unlike Europe and parts of MENA, African municipalities do **not** pay gate fees to waste processors. This changes the EAAS revenue model:

| Revenue Source | Europe | MENA | Africa |
|---------------|--------|------|--------|
| Gate/tipping fees | Yes ($30-80/t) | Some markets | **No** |
| RDF sales to cement | Yes | Yes | **Yes — primary revenue** |
| Government waste contracts | Varies | Varies | Possible (LAWMA in Lagos, HYSACAM in Cameroon) |
| Carbon credits | Emerging | Emerging | **Available** (diverting waste from open dumps) |

**In Africa, 100% of EAAS revenue comes from RDF sales to the cement factory.** There is no tipping fee subsidy. The economics must work purely on the coal-to-RDF arbitrage. They do — but the margin is thinner than in markets with tipping fees.

**Upside:** Carbon credit revenue from diverting waste from open dumps could add $2-5/tonne of MSW processed. This should be factored into the financial model.
