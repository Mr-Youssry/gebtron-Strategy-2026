## Screening Criteria

Countries were screened using criteria from Strategy Session 3 (January 2026):

| Criterion | Threshold | Rationale |
|-----------|-----------|-----------|
| Population | >10 million | Ensures sufficient MSW volume for RDF feedstock |
| Energy subsidies | Minimal or none | Market-based pricing = EAAS immediately profitable |
| Cement industry | Growing | Demand for alternative fuels exists |
| Political stability | Sufficient for long-term investment | 15-year EAAS contracts need safe environment |
| Competition | Low from European/Chinese AF OEMs | First-mover advantage available |

## Countries Eliminated at Screening

| Country | Reason | Details |
|---------|--------|---------|
| **Rwanda** | MSW volume too small | Kigali generates 290-440K t/yr; EAAS needs 800K minimum. Best governance in Africa but market too small for standard line. |
| **Angola** | Cheap energy (oil producer) | Domestic energy prices undercut EAAS value proposition. Heavy subsidies (<$0.03/kWh electricity). |
| **DRC** | Governance/security risk | Kinshasa has massive MSW (2.2M+ t/yr) but institutional capacity among lowest in Africa. 99% hydropower = cheap energy. |
| **Zambia** | Below MSW threshold + cheap hydro | Lusaka at 550-730K t/yr. Very low electricity (<$0.03/kWh) due to hydropower. |
| **Zimbabwe** | Below MSW threshold + instability | Harare at 550-730K t/yr. Extreme currency instability. Pomona WtE deal controversy. |
| **Mozambique** | Cheap energy (coal exporter) | Domestic coal available at $100-120/t — lowest in Africa. Limited cement market. |
| **Uganda** | Borderline volume + waste quality | 730K-1.1M t/yr. Very high organic content (65-75%) — lowest calorific value in the study. Kiteezi landfill collapse (Aug 2024). |

## Scoring Methodology

### Seven Criteria with Weighted Scores

| Criterion | Weight | Why This Weight | Scoring (1-5) |
|-----------|--------|-----------------|---------------|
| **Cement Demand** | ×5 | No cement = no RDF buyer | 1=tiny, 3=moderate, 5=massive (Nigeria) |
| **MSW Volume** | ×5 | No waste = no feedstock | 1=below threshold, 3=meets threshold, 5=abundant |
| **Energy Pricing** | ×4 | Higher energy cost = stronger EAAS savings | 1=heavily subsidized, 3=moderate, 5=market-based + high |
| **Political Stability** | ×4 | 15-year contracts need safe environment | 1=conflict, 3=stable but risks, 5=very stable |
| **Low Competition** | ×3 | First-mover advantage | 1=crowded, 3=moderate, 5=wide open |
| **Currency Stability** | ×4 | 15-year contract denominated in local currency is existential risk | 1=extreme volatility, 3=moderate, 5=pegged to EUR/USD |
| **Ease of Entry** | ×3 | Regulatory friction adds cost and time | 1=very complex, 3=moderate, 5=simple/100% foreign |

**Maximum possible score:** 5 × (5+5+4+4+3+4+3) = 5 × 28 = **140**

## Why Senegal Ranks First (Not Nigeria)

This is counterintuitive. Nigeria is 13× Senegal's population and has 7× the cement capacity. But for a 15-year EAAS contract, the ranking reflects **contract survivability**, not market size:

| Factor | Senegal | Nigeria |
|--------|---------|---------|
| **Currency risk over 15 years** | Zero. CFA = EUR peg since 1945. | NGN lost 70%+ in 2023-24. A 15-year contract denominated in NGN is a bet on Nigerian monetary policy. |
| **First customer** | Sococim has IFC-backed EUR 242M for 70% AF — they are actively buying what Gebtron sells. | Dangote has 15 AF systems but procurement is competitive. Geocycle (Holcim/Huaxin) is an incumbent. |
| **Registration** | APIX one-stop, 6 days, 100% foreign ownership. | Triple registration (CAC + NIPC + Business Permit), NGN 100M minimum capital. |
| **DFI co-financing** | IFC is already financing AF in Senegal. Proparco (French DFI) is natural partner. | IFC invested $500M in BUA but not AF-specific. |
| **Scalability** | CFA zone = Cote d'Ivoire, Cameroon, Mali, Burkina Faso all use same currency/regulatory framework. | Nigeria is its own regulatory island. |

**Nigeria is the larger prize, but Senegal is the better first move.** Prove the model in Senegal with IFC backing and CFA currency safety, then use that reference to enter Nigeria from a position of strength.

## The Shortlist — Recommended Sequence

| Phase | Country | Timeline | Rationale |
|-------|---------|----------|-----------|
| **1** | **Senegal** | 2028 | CFA zone, IFC-backed Sococim demand, easy entry, Dakar Fort |
| **2** | **Cote d'Ivoire** | 2028-29 | Same CFA zone, CIMAF HQ (13 plants, zero AF), Pfeiffer already has a client |
| **3** | **Kenya** | 2029 | East Africa anchor, Bamburi 98% AF proof point, best regulatory framework |
| **4** | **Tanzania** | 2029-30 | Extension of Kenya presence, surplus production, Amsons/Dangote demand |
| **5** | **Cameroon** | 2029-30 | CFA zone, HYSACAM waste partnership, CIMENCAM (Holcim) |
| **6** | **Nigeria** | 2030+ | The whale — Dangote 12-plant pipeline, largest market, free zone entity |

**Constraint (WT-3):** This sequence respects the SWOT-TOWS finding that Gebtron cannot fight on 5 fronts with a 15-person team. EAAS must be proven in MENA (2026-27) before Africa entry begins.
